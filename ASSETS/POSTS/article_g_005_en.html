The Main Problem Is to Work With Ordinals In The Form \( \Psi_\pi^0(\alpha) \)

${S(8)}I have good news and bad news. The good news is, the FS (Fundamental Sequence) of the other functions (\( \varphi \), \( \Omega \) and \( \Xi \)) are quite easy.

${S(8)}The FS of the Veblen function \( \varphi_\alpha(\beta) \) is well-known among the googology community. You can check out my previous article and find my definition of the FS (Line 04.).

${S(8)}The FS of the \( \Omega_\alpha \) function (or \( \aleph_\alpha \) function) is also well-known and pretty easy:

\( \begin{align} \Omega_{\alpha+1}[n] & := n \\ \Omega_{\alpha}[n] & := \Omega_{\alpha[n]} \quad(\alpha\in Lim) \end{align} \)

${S(8)}The FS of the \( \Xi(\alpha) \) function is also pretty easy because it always returns a regular ordinal:

\( \Xi(\alpha)[n]:=n \)

${S(8)}And even for the \( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha) \) function, if we have \( \xi>0 \), then it still always returns a regular ordinal:

\( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha)[n]:=n\quad(\xi>0) \)

${S(8)}Which means, we just have to focus on the ordinals In The Form \( \Psi_\pi^0(\alpha) \).

${S(8)}But the bad news is, they're extremely difficult to work with. The first reason is this OCF doesn't work the same as the one we're familiar with like Buchholz's OCF${C(3)} and Rathjen's little \( \psi \). For example: (Rathjen's little \( \psi \) is noted as \( \rho \) for specification)

\( \begin{align} \psi_0(\Omega+1) & = sup\{0,\psi_0(\Omega),\psi_0(\Omega)*2,\psi_0(\Omega)*3,\cdots\} \\ \rho_\Omega(\Omega+1) & = sup\{\rho_\Omega(\Omega),\varphi_{\rho_\Omega(\Omega)}(1),\varphi_{\varphi_{\rho_\Omega(\Omega)}(1)}(0),\varphi_{\varphi_{\varphi_{\rho_\Omega(\Omega)}(1)}(0)}(0),\cdots\} \end{align} \)

${S(8)}Since Rathjen's capital \( \Psi \) also uses Veblen function, it's natural to expect this:

\( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1)=sup\{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega),\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1),\varphi_{\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1)}(0),\varphi_{\varphi_{\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1)}(0)}(0),\cdots\} \)

${S(8)}But ACTUALLY:

\( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1)=sup\{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega))),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)))),\cdots\} \)

${S(8)}Like, "What the heck? OCFs are supposed to be monotonically increasing! You can't just plug in a smaller ordinal and get a larger ordinal! And also, \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)) \) is not in normal form and it's equal to \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega) \)!"

Veblen-Like OCFs Like This Are Hard to Work With

${S(8)}Unfortunately, ladies and gentlemen, this is how this OCF works. Rathjen's capital \( \Psi \) is not monotonically increasing as the OCFs we're familiar with. Also, \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)) \) is actually in normal form (\( \in T(K) \)).

${S(8)}Also, let's check out these examples: (I'm using a different FS for the Veblen function from the one I previously defined)

\( \begin{align} \varphi_1(1) & = sup\{\varphi_1(0),\varphi_0(\varphi_1(0)+1),\varphi_0(\varphi_0(\varphi_1(0)+1)),\varphi_0(\varphi_0(\varphi_0(\varphi_1(0)+1))),\cdots\} \\ \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1) & = sup\{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega))),\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)))),\cdots\} \end{align} \)

${S(8)}If we write \( \varphi_\alpha(\beta) \) as \( \varphi(\Omega\alpha+\beta) \), we have: (Although this form is ill-defined)

\( \varphi(\Omega+1)=sup\{\varphi(\Omega),\varphi(\varphi(\Omega)+1),\varphi(\varphi(\varphi(\Omega)+1)),\varphi(\varphi(\varphi(\varphi(\Omega)+1))),\cdots\} \)

${S(8)}You can see that Veblen function and Rathjen's capital \( \Psi \) are similar in some way. I call OCFs like these "Veblen-like" and call the monotonically increasing OCFs like Buchholz's OCF and Rathjen's little \( \psi \) "Buchholz-like" (Although more rigorous definitions are required, For example, whether it's monotonically increasing). Actually, there's also another Veblen-like OCF called Weiermann's \( \vartheta \) function${C(4)} (It's actually a curly Theta). This OCF is actually much easier (and thus weaker) so check it out if you want to understand more about OCFs like these.

${S(8)}So, what's the problem? They just work different and look kinda cool. Well, again, unfortunately, it causes a lot of problems. Since Rathjen's capital \( \Psi \) is not monotonically increasing, it's really easy to have some "forgotten ordinals".

${S(8)}Let's recap the definition of FS. The FS of an ordinal \( \alpha \) is a sequence \( a[n] \) that:

${S(8)}1. Every element is less than \( \alpha \): \( \forall n\in cof(\alpha)(\alpha[n]<\alpha) \)

${S(8)}2. Is monotonically (or even strictly) increasing: \( \forall m,n\in cof(\alpha)(m<n\Rightarrow\alpha[m]\le\alpha[n]) \) (Or \( \alpha[m]<\alpha[n] \))

${S(8)}3. The sequence approaches to \( \alpha \): \( sup\{\alpha[n]|n\in cof(\alpha)\}=\alpha \)


${S(8)}It's easy to make sure that the condition 1. and 2. are satisfied because we can write a code that compares 2 ordinals. But it's impossible (Or very difficult, at least) to verify whether a sequence approaches to some ordinal. There might always be some "forgotten ordinals" between the ordinal WE THINK the sequence approaches to and the ordinal the sequence ACTUALLY approaches to. Being careful enough is always too difficult.

${S(8)}For example, If we define the FS of \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1) \) in such way:

\( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1)=sup\{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega),\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1),\varphi_{\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1)}(0),\varphi_{\varphi_{\varphi_{\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)}(1)}(0)}(0),\cdots\} \)

${S(8)}How do we write a code to determine that there are ordinals between the limit of this sequence (Which is actually \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega)) \)) and \( \Psi_\Omega^0(\Omega+1) \) and thus this sequence is wrong? Because we can't just brute force, it will LITERALLY take FOREVER!!!

${S(8)}Well, after some analysis that I THINK is enough, it's still possible to write the correct expansion rule, which I did. It's still easy enough for some relatively small ordinals. But as the ordinal goes up, it gets more difficult to analyze and easier to mess up.

Some Misconceptions About Whether A Term "Succeeds to Collapse"

${S(8)}If you see the definition as follows:

\( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha):=min(\{\rho\in M^\xi\cup\pi:C(\alpha,\rho)=\rho\land\pi,\alpha\in C(\alpha,\rho)\}\cup\{\pi\})\quad(\xi\le\alpha) \)

${S(8)}You might think there are cases that \( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha)=\pi \). And actually, this is true. This is what I call the term "Fails to Collapse". And I call the terms \( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha) \) that \( \Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha)<\pi \) "Succeeds to Collapse".

${S(8)}Since the whole ordinal system \( T(K) \) well-founded, meaning that it has a least element, then the set \( A=\{\alpha|\Psi_\pi^\xi(\alpha)<\pi\} \) also has a least element which I call the "Collapsing Limit" and denote here as \( \lambda_\pi \). To get such limit, I just had to see how the cardinal \( \pi \) is composed of, for example:

\( \begin{align} \lambda_{\Omega} & = 0 \\ \lambda_{\Omega_2} & = 0 \\ \lambda_{\Omega_{\Psi_\Omega^0(0)+1}} & = 0+1=1 \\ \lambda_\Xi & = 1+1=2 \\ \lambda_{\Xi_2} & = 2+1=3 \\ \lambda_{\Xi(K)} & = K+1 \\ \lambda_{\Psi_{\Xi_2}^1(\Psi_{\Xi_2}^0(\Xi_2))} & = \Xi_2+1 \end{align} \)

${S(8)}But actually, some of the above are wrong. According to Definition 5.1(T6) on Rathjen's paper, \( A=\{\alpha|\pi\in C(\alpha,\pi)\} \), and he also defined a neat function \( K_\delta(\alpha) \) such that \( \alpha\in C(\gamma,\delta) \) IF AND ONLY IF \( K_\delta(\alpha)<\gamma \). In fact, a similar function is also defined in Rathjen's PDF on Rathjen's little \( \psi \)${C(2)}. I should point out that this function returns a set of ordinals, and \( K_\delta(\alpha)<\gamma \) means \( \gamma \) is greater than EVERY element in the set. And now we have \( \lambda_\pi=max(K_\pi(\pi))+1 \) (If \( K_\pi(\pi)=\{\} \) then \( \lambda_\pi=0 \)). Using this function, I figured out that my initial observation was wrong.
