${S(8)}So far, most of the articles I've posted about googology are about large ordinals. And I didn't talk about actual numbers that often. This is because with the ordinals and their FSes (Fundamental Sequences) well-defined, it is really easy to define a large number well. But since I've had some difficulties and setbacks while studying complex OCFs (Ordinal Collapsing Functions), I decide to change my mood and post something else that only scratches the surface of OCFs.

${S(8)}In this article, I'm going to share my standard to classify large numbers, i.e. "Googological Ruler", which is a measure of how far we have gone in this journey of large numbers. We all have to start from "small" numbers that we use in our daily life. And as the number goes up ridiculously quickly, it's quite easy to get lost and ask something like "What am I doing?". As milestones are set, we know where we are, how to proceed, and when to celebrate. And after I show my googological ruler, I'm going to show my statistics on the distribution of numbers on Googology Wiki by level on this ruler.

Some More Explanation

${S(8)}Before we classify numbers, the first thing we need to do is to apply a "Normalization Function" to the number as follows: (That's right, we also consider real numbers and complex numbers. We can even consider quaternions, octonions, sedenions and so on if we want.)

${S(8)}\( f:\mathbb{C}\rightarrow[1,+\infty) \)

${S(8)}\( f(z)=\begin{cases} f(|z|) & z\notin[0,+\infty) \\ 1 & z=0 \\ 1/z & z\in(0,1) \\ z & z\in[1,+\infty) \end{cases} \)

${S(8)}This is because I see a lot of people creating numbers as SMALL as possible. But since these numbers are usually created using large numbers that are relatively hard to define, I think their level should remain the same after the extra steps that makes the number extremely small. But if you plug a large number into a weird function that nullifies the normalization like \( g(x)=1+1/x \), Then there's no saving this number, get back to the lowest level. This is the reason why my googological ruler starts from the number 1.

${S(8)}After the normalization, we start by comparing the normalized number to the lower bound of level 0. If the normalized number is greater than or equal to the lower bound of the current level, we move to the next level and rinse and repeat. If we encounter the level whose lower bound is greater than the normalized number, then we get the previous level as a result.

${S(8)}Besides the main classifications above, we also have subclasses. We follow the instruction below in order. We denote the number as \( n \), \( \mathfrak{R}(n) \) as the real part of \( n \), \( \mathfrak{I}(n) \) as the imaginary part of \( n \) and \( |n| \) as the modulus of \( n \).

${S(8)}01.If \( n=0 \), we add "Z" (Zero) in the end.

${S(8)}02.If \( |n|<1 \), we add "M" (Micro) in the end.

${S(8)}03.If \( \mathfrak{R}(n)<0 \) and \( \mathfrak{I}(n)=0 \), we add "N" (Negative) in the end.

${S(8)}04.If \( \mathfrak{R}(n)=0 \) and \( \mathfrak{I}(n)>0 \), we add "I" (Imaginary) in the end.

Conclusion

${S(8)}And there you have it, my googological ruler and statistics. In fact, I follow some principles when creating this ruler like making the numbers we use in our daily life take up a lot of levels, making a lot of space for numbers that can be handled by the Peano Arithmetic, making space for something we don't know and so on.

${S(8)}Although studying Stegert's OCF doesn't help us to beat the top numbers on the internet, it does help us to beat the top COMPUTABLE numbers on the internet. We can all learn uncomputable functions before learning complex computable functions that are much weaker, but we cannot easily know how to create computable functions under uncomputable systems like Turing Machine, Lambda Calculus and so on.

${S(8)}Googology is like BLACKJACK. In blackjack, you want your number to get close to some number but not to surpass it. In the game, we want to reach 21 points or as close as possible, or should we say we want to get as close to 22 points as possible but not to reach or surpass it. In googology, we want a number that is as close to \( \omega \) as possible but not to reach or surpass it. Sometimes, we want an ordinal that is as close to \( \varepsilon_0 \), \( \omega_1^{CK} \), \( \Omega \) and so on as possible but not to reach or surpass it. So, it should be reasonable to understand computable functions that grows as fast as possible but not uncomputable.

${S(8)}Furthermore, studying extremely complex OCFs like Stegert's OCF helps to understand more complicated and potential systems like BMS (Bashicu Matrix System) and Taranovsky's C${C(8)}. I see a lot of people using some unknown OCFs or Ordinal Notations to analyze these systems, this is probably due to lack of knowledge of complex OCFs. If they understood complex OCFs like Rathjen's little \( \psi \), Rathjen's capital \( \Psi \), their analysis would be more robust. In fact, I see a lot of people in the googology community who understands Taranovsky's C and has done some analyses that I completely don't understand. Since I currently don't understand Taranovsky's C, I'm really curious about their knowledge of this system.

${S(8)}Speaking of Stegert's OCF, I claimed that this OCF is the "Current final boss of OCFs" on my ruler. Actually, this might not be true. I see an ordinal written as \( \psi_{\omega_1^{CK}}(\varepsilon_{\mathbb{I}+1}) \) at the end of the table on the "Ordinal analysis" page on Wikipedia. This seems to be the PTO (Proof-Theoretic Ordinal) of \( KP+\Pi_1-Collection \)${C(9)}, which as the table suggests, seems to be even larger than \( LSO=\mathfrak{P}_{(\omega^+;P_0;\epsilon;\epsilon;0)}^{\varepsilon_{\Upsilon+1}} \). This ordinal is due to Arai, who also created Arai's \( \psi \) function${C(10)}. But when I see the webpage of the paper about this ordinal, it says that the newest version of this paper is WITHDRAWN. I don't know what's happening. Does this new OCF have problems? Or is it actually going to BEAT Stegert's OCF? Without enough information, I decide to ignore this new OCF for now. Maybe I'm just in denial and don't want to go any further.

${S(8)}EDIT 2024/05/25: It turn's out that the claim above isn't true! Arai has published an upgraded version of his withdrawn OCF with a new large ordinal written as \( \psi_{\omega_1^{CK}}(\mathbb{I}_\omega) \) and it's claimed to be the PTO of \( Z_2 \) (not \( \Phi_2(2) \)) or the WHOLE second-order arithmetic!${C(11)} (With Dependent Choice Schema. I don't know whether this theory and \( Z_2 \) alone are equiconsistent) If this paper contains no critical mistakes, then this will be the true current final boss of OCFs!
